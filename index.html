Omoide Yokocho — Memory Lane
Google Maps

A narrow alley of 20+ tiny yakitori bars running directly behind Shinjuku Station's west side. Bare bulbs, open charcoal grills, a cloud of chicken smoke, and seats for maybe 8 people per bar. Order negima (chicken-leek skewers), tsukune (chicken meatball), and skin-on wings. Cold Sapporo draft. This is one of the best cheap eats in all of Tokyo — ¥2,000–3,000 fills you up completely. No reservations, just walk in. If one's full, the next is two steps away.

Getting There

Golden Gai — After Dinner
Google Maps

Six narrow alleyways, ~200 tiny bars, each seating 5–10 people. Every bar has its own identity — jazz, horror, punk, retro anime, classic film. Cover charge ¥500–1,000 is normal. Walk slowly until one calls to you — peek in, and go in if the vibe fits. Best experienced after 9pm when locals fill the seats alongside travellers. Easy to end up deep in conversation until 1am. One of the genuinely irreplaceable Tokyo experiences.

Meiji Jingu Shrine
Google Maps

Exit Harajuku Station from the Omote-sando exit and walk straight into the forested approach — a 10-minute path through dense cedar trees that immediately kills the city noise. The shrine itself is dedicated to Emperor Meiji; the grounds include a garden, an inner complex with two forested courtyards, and walls hung with ema wooden wishing plaques. Before 9am it's quiet enough to feel meditative. The sake barrel collection near the main complex includes French Bordeaux barrels (gifts from winemakers) alongside Japanese sake — an unexpectedly telling detail about Japan's global connections.

Getting There

Harajuku Station → Omote-sando Exit → walk straight, the torii gate is directly ahead. Follow the gravel path 10 min to the shrine complex. Walking route

Takeshita Street → Omotesando
Google Maps

Walk the contrast: Takeshita-dori is 400m of packed youth fashion, crepe stands, and kawaii accessories — chaotic and worth 20 minutes. Then walk south and emerge onto Omotesando — a wide, tree-lined avenue with flagship architecture stores (Comme des Garçons by Future Systems, Prada by Herzog & de Meuron, Louis Vuitton by Aoki). Omotesando Hills is a Tadao Ando-designed mall with a spiralling atrium — enter just for the building. Cat Street behind it runs independent streetwear and design boutiques.

Shibuya Crossing + Shibuya Sky
Google Maps

Stand in the middle of the scramble crossing during a green light — 3,000 people moving simultaneously in every direction. Then go above it: Shibuya Sky at 229m is an open-air rooftop deck on Shibuya Scramble Square with glass perimeter panels and 360° views including Mount Fuji (southwest, on clear days). Book in advance for golden hour — 4–6pm — the city shifts amber and the crossing below becomes a light show. After dark the neon and office towers make it equally worth it.

Shimokitazawa — Records + Bars
Google Maps

Tokyo's most characterful neighbourhood. Independent record shops (Disk Union, Flash Disc Ranch), vintage clothing, tiny jazz bars, and coffee that takes its time. Key spots: BONUS TRACK for curated shops and small restaurants; Reload next door for a rotating art installation and food. Very local, very Tokyo — completely unlike Shibuya or Shinjuku two stations back. Allow 2+ hours just to wander. Stay for dinner here — the izakayas and craft beer spots are relaxed and good.

Park Strategy — How to Do This Right
01The moment you enter: open Tokyo Disney Resort app, buy Disney Premier Access (DPA) for Tower of Terror immediately — before walking anywhere.
02Then run to Soaring: Fantastic Flight (straight ahead, Mediterranean Harbor) — rope-drop this standby. By 10am it's 90+ minutes.
03Buy your next DPA 1 hour after last purchase — use it for Journey to the Center of the Earth. It regularly hits 200-min waits; DPA cuts it to 15.
04Eat at 11am or after 2pm. Set up Disney Mobile Order in the app before entering — eliminates food queuing completely.
05Fantasy Springs (Frozen, Tangled, Peter Pan) is on standby now. Hit it 2–4pm when morning crowds thin out.
06Stay for the nighttime show — Mediterranean Harbor projection + pyro spectacular. After it ends, queues collapse. Re-ride before closing.

Tower of Terror — Via DPA
Google Maps

DisneySea's version is the darkest, most elaborately themed Tower of Terror of any Disney park in the world. Based on an Indiana Jones-adjacent storyline, you enter the crumbling Hotel Hightower and descend into a haunted elevator that free-falls multiple times in rapid succession. The building exterior — 40 storeys of crumbling stone, vines, and atmospheric decay — is a masterpiece. Standby hits 180 min+ by mid-morning. DPA is ~¥1,500 and completely worth it.

Google Maps

Senso-ji Temple — Before 8am
Google Maps

Tokyo's oldest temple (founded 628 AD) — and one of the world's most-visited sites, which means timing is everything. Before 8am the Kaminarimon gate is empty, the incense smoke drifts across the courtyard in still air, and the main hall is occupied only by locals praying. The 200m Nakamise-dori approach (market stalls) is shuttered, letting you see the Edo-period buildings they occupy. Try omikuji fortune papers (¥100 — shake the box until a numbered stick falls out, find the corresponding drawer). By 8:30am tour groups arrive and the experience shifts entirely.

Sumida River Walk → Tokyo Skytree
Google Maps

Walk 15 min south along the Sumida River from Senso-ji — the Skytree becomes visible almost immediately and grows as you approach. The Tembo Deck at 350m gives the complete Tokyo panorama; the Tembo Galleria at 450m is a glass-floored spiral walkway at the highest point. Book online before arriving to skip the queue — walk-up waits are substantial, especially weekends. The Solamachi shopping complex at the base has 300+ shops and a good range of restaurants if you need a break.

Akihabara — Electric Town
Google Maps

Multi-storey buildings blasting J-pop, covered in anime billboards, stacked floor-to-floor with electronics, manga, trading cards, gaming hardware, and figurines. Key stops: Yodobashi Akiba (8 floors of electronics — tax-free for tourists) for anything tech; Mandarake for vintage collectibles and old manga; Super Potato for retro gaming (Famicom, SNES, arcade sticks); Pokémon Center Akihabara for exclusive merch. Even if you're not into anime culture, the density and energy of the place is worth an hour.

Getting There

Google Maps

Google Maps

Remove shoes and socks at the entrance and walk through rooms where art installations respond to your movement in real time. Essential Tips

Before you land

Getting Around

  • Suica IC card — tap on trains, buses, and conbini
  • Google Maps is perfect for Tokyo transit routing
  • Shin-Kiba has JR Keiyo + Rinkai + Yurakucho Lines
  • Last trains run ~midnight–12:30am on main lines
  • Taxis work but trains are almost always faster

Book in Advance

  • teamLab Planets — 1–2 weeks ahead, sells out
  • DisneySea tickets — Tokyo Disney Resort app
  • Shibuya Sky — book a time slot online
  • Ristorante di Canaletto — in-park app on the day
  • Final yakiniku dinner — reserve ahead

Money

  • Japan is still heavily cash-based in 2026
  • 7-Eleven ATM — no fees with foreign cards
  • Tax-free on purchases ¥5,000+ (claim at airport)
  • Weak yen makes everything very affordable

Pack + Weather

  • Late May / June: humid, 22–28°C
  • Light rain jacket — rainy season begins June
  • Comfortable shoes — 15,000+ steps daily
  • Google Translate camera mode is essential
